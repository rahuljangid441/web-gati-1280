INDO NEPAL HALDIA
ACCESS CONTROLLED CORRIDOR

Across the corridor that stretches from the international borders of Nepal to the EXIM gateways in the eastern parts of the country, PM GatiShakti
National Master Plan enabled quick assessment of clearances, reduction of ecological impact and integrated planning for economic
and social connectivity, thereby maximising logistics efficiency and boosting employment.

Overall project scope

673 km (418.18 miles) 6-lane access-controlled corridor of Expressways and National Highways

Expected completion date:

2027-28

Cost outlay:

Rs. 30,233 crores ($ 302.33 million)

BACKGROUND AND VISION FOR THE PROJECT

From the busy Eastern ports plying trade and commerce to the hinterlands of Bihar and Jharkhand and across the international borders to neighbouring Nepal, this path-breaking expressway and highway is expected to drive both increased regional connectivity and access to tourism. The corridor will provide seamless connectivity between Nepal (Kathmandu & Birgunj via Raxaul), Bihar (Patna), Jharkhand (Dhanbad), West Bengal (Asansol, Durgapur, Kolkata) and Haldia Port without congesting the city of Kolkata.

Be it the world-famous opals or silk textiles of Patna or coal from the coal capital of India (Dhanbad), this expressway will provide an easy route to the movement of these goods.
The artisans and workers from the coal mines will be able to use this expressway to reach their homes in Kolkata or Gorakhpur in just a matter of hours.

This corridor, along with the other proposed high-speed corridors such as the Gorakhpur - Siliguri corridor, the Gorakhpur – Bareilly corridor, and the Varanasi-Ranchi-Kolkata, will interconnect economic centres of Northern and Eastern India, apart from 4 tourist spots, aspirational districts and tribal districts that need development. It will help improve logistics efficiency, reduce carbon emissions and boost employment in the region.

THE GATISHAKTI EDGE AND APPROACH

The tools of the PM GatiShakti National Master Plan facilitated comprehensive infrastructure planning and the optimisation of alignment. This encompassed the coordinated acquisition of shared right of way (RoW) for various connectivity infrastructures, such as railways, urban mass transit, trunk utilities, optical fibre cables, power, water, sewage lines, and more, along greenfield corridors as relevant. Leveraging the comprehensive geological data within the National Master Plan, including eco-sensitive zones, enabled the identification of optimal alignments to minimise ecological impact.The requisites
for developing the Indo-Nepal Border-Haldia access-controlled corridor, concerning clearances from other infrastructure and utility agencies, were identified through the integration of GIS information on railway and utility lines within the PM GatiShakti National Master Plan portal. This contributed to streamlining the land acquisition process. Seamless connectivity was precisely planned across over 20 economic nodes, spanning from electronic manufacturing clusters to fishing hubs, food parks, textile clusters, pharma zones, and tribal districts, all complementing other transportation modes
Through this integrated planning approach, 15 districts across Bihar (East Champaran, Muzaffarpur, Vaishali, Samastipur, Patna, Sheikhpura, Lakhisarai, Jamui), Jharkhand (Dhanbad, Deoghar, Jamtara), and West Bengal (Puruliya, Bankura, Paschim Medinipur, Purba Medinipur) were interconnected.
Moreover, it enabled the evaluation of multimodal transportation improvements by enhancing access to 10 major railway stations, 3 airports, 2 multimodal logistics parks (MMLPs), and 1 port within the region.

THE ROUTE ALIGNMENT OF INDO NEPAL BORDER-
HALDIA EXPRESSWAY ON THE PM GATISHAKTI PORTAL

The portal effectively mapped the corridor's intersections with the National Highway network at 15 points, the state highway network at 12 points, railway lines at 18 points, and water bodies at 41 locations. Notably, the project initially encountered a conflict with a coal mining area in its alignment, posing potential adverse effects during expressway construction. The Coal Ministry and the Ministry of Road Transportation and Highways worked together to identify an 80 m corridor for alignment within the Coal mining area that would benefit both. Three alignments were chalked out initially.

The final alignment was chosen based on the assessment that it could serve as an additional economic hub and ensure clear movement of coal from this reserve all the way to Haldia port and other parts of the country and abroad. This project is also a benchmark for regional cooperation, in this case, enhancing connectivity with our neighbouring country, Nepal.

THE EXPECTED IMPACT

Currently, at the conceptualisation stage, the completed project will enable an 18% length reduction from 819 km (509 miles) in the original alignment to 673 km (420 miles) by ensuring an optimised alignment following the GatiShakti principles. The average speed is set to increase from about ~40-50 kmph to ~80-100 kmph, reducing travel time by 11-12 hours. The reduced distance is expected to lead to a ~11.26 crore (112.6 million) litre fuel savings and carbon dioxide reduction of ~27 crore (270 million) kg annually.

With the increased economic and social activity, the corridor is also expected to have direct and indirect employment of ~38.5 crore (385 million) person days with induced employment of ~191.6 crore (1,916 million) person-days.
