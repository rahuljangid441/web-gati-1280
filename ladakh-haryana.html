GREEN ENERGY CORRIDOR
BETWEEN LADAKH AND HARYANA

PM GatiShakti National Master Plan helped in planning a historic green corridor that will help India reach its renewable energy goal of generating 500 gigawatts of renewable energy by 2030, leveraging the vast solar energy potential in the high-altitude regions of Ladakh within the majestic Himalayas.

Overall project scope

13 GW non-fossil energy (solar and wind) power generation

Expected completion date:

2029-30

Cost outlay:

Rs. 80,000 crores ($ 9 billion)

BACKGROUND AND VISION FOR THE PROJECT

During his Independence Day speech 2020, the Hon’ble Prime announced plans for a solar park in the pristine stretches of Ladakh. The picturesque valley that attracts thousands of tourists every year for its serene and breathtaking beauty also presents a unique opportunity for future-facing renewable energy for our country.

After undertaking a detailed survey, the Ministry of New and Renewable Energy estimated that there is a 32 GW potential for solar and wind generation in this locale.In the initial phase, a 13 GW (9GW Solar & 4GW Wind) renewable energy project along with a 12 GWh
Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) for grid integration at Kaithal in Haryana is proposed to connect to the existing national grid. This project comprises a state-of-the-art High Voltage Direct Current system based on Voltage Source Converters (VSC-HVDC) terminals of 5000 MW each at Pang (Leh) and at Kaithal (Haryana) along with a 480 km (298 miles) long HVDC line between the two terminals.

It is one of the first and only projects of this magnitude to be conceptualised and planned in such difficult geographic and climatic conditions.

THE GATISHAKTI EDGE AND APPROACH

The PM GatiShakti approach transformed the project's execution at such a high height of 4,800 meters amidst adverse climate circumstances where temperatures dropped to -45°C and air density was drastically reduced. This project had multiple topographical challenges, such as interactions of ultraviolet and cosmic rays with electronic components, the absence of meteorological data for civil design, and proximity to a sensitive military zone along an international border. Through the integration of PM GatiShakti's GIS maps, gaps in establishing an environmentally sustainable pathway were identified and bridged, streamlining planning processes across multiple governmental departments and ministries. The intricacies of transporting equipment like solar panels and inverters
were addressed, while the Border Roads Organisation focused on enhancing road infrastructure to accommodate the transportation of heavy machinery. The synergy of infrastructure planning from various Ministries on the National Master Plan platform expedited clearance procedures and surveys.The multi-level planning of infrastructure available digitally on the National Master Plan platform from various Ministries has expedited the process of clearances, surveys, etc. The PM GatiShakti NMP platform played a pivotal role in minimising interference with forests and wildlife habitats by transmission lines, thereby reducing costs. The National Master Plan under PM GatiShakti facilitated the selection of an optimal route for reversing transmission lines, linking the new renewable energy source to the grid.
This selection was influenced by the unique challenges posed by high altitudes, low temperatures, strong wind speeds, and challenging right-of-way conditions in the area. Without this comprehensive planning tool, it would have been arduous to identify a route that satisfied both technical and environmental prerequisites. The portal also facilitated the identification of shared pathways for roads, transmission lines, and other utilities near the transmission system. This approach aimed to mitigate the project's ecological impact and enhance its overall efficiency in construction and maintenance. Furthermore, the PM GatiShakti approach played a crucial role in mobilising the
infrastructure required for transmitting energy from Kaithal to nearby industrial centres and economic hubs. Both ends of the project, in Ladakh and Kaithal, were interconnected through a multimodal network. This included pathways from Leh airport to Una railway station and National Highway 3 linking to Manali on the Ladakh side, extending to Chandigarh airport, Kurukshetra railway junction, and National Highway 52A connecting Punjab to Karnataka. Similarly, State Highway 11 on the Haryana side established connections to four of Haryana's economic focal points: Fatehabad, Sirsa, Hisar, and Mahendragarh.

THE EXPECTED IMPACT

This project is a step towards the Government of India's goal of 500 GW of non-fossil fuel capacity by the year 2030 with significant reductions in carbon emissions. It is expected to reduce carbon emissions by up to 26 million tonnes per year. This will directly contribute to our nation's green energy goals while contributing to local employment generation of up to 3 million person-hours for 3 years and 0.5 million person-hours for operations and maintenance. The transmission work by the Power Grid will employ over 1,000 people. It will drive logistics efficiency as the transmission system will connect Ladakh to Haryana, which, in turn, will be connected to the national grid.

Not just that, it will drastically reduce power generation cost from Rs. 18/unit through current diesel power generation to Rs. 3.75/unit post-completion of the project in Ladakh and ~Rs. 4.63/unit in Haryana. A positive impact of telecom infrastructure with satellite phones and telephones and civic infrastructure is expected because of this in the Ladakh region and Jammu and Kashmir, leading to better ease of living for people living there. The country will experience significant cost reductions because of supplying green power to the electric grid. It will contribute to the creation of employment opportunities as well as the overall economic development of Ladakh by providing cost-effective and reliable power for economic growth.
